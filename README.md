# ZX Spectrum Repair: A Beginners Guide

## 1. Board Identification

Before anything else, identify which issue of board you have. This is silkscreened on the PCB itself, usually near the bottom edge. Boards were swapped, recased, and re-shelled throughout the lifetime of these machines, so never assume the case style tells you which board is inside. Always open it up and read the silkscreen.

The original Sinclair 16K/48K boards you will encounter are:

- **Issue 1**: Exceptionally rare, large aluminium heatsink, distinctive layout. Highly collectable.
- **Issue 2**: Early production, common.
- **Issue 3, 3B**: The most common 48K boards.
- **Issue 4A, 4B & 4S**: Production refinements, still using the same RAM and power arrangement.
- **Issue 5**: Ultra rare. Short production run, a genuine collector piece that changes hands for thousands.
- **Issue 6A**: Later production with a second logic array controlling upper RAM.

All 16K and 48K Spectrums share the same fundamental power arrangement: an unregulated 9V DC input, a 7805 generating +5V, and a small switching stage around TR4 and TR5 that produces -5V and +12V for the 4116 DRAM chips. The three-rail arrangement is universal across the 16K and 48K range.

For later machines (128K toastrack, +2 in the Amstrad grey case, +2A, +2B, +3) the board design is different and falls outside the scope of this guide.

## 2. Tools You Need

At minimum:

- A digital multimeter with continuity beep and diode test
- Medium Philips screwdriver, small flat-blade screwdriver
- A soldering iron (25 to 40 watts) and decent solder
- Desoldering braid or a solder sucker
- Isopropyl alcohol and cotton buds

Useful but not essential for a beginner:

- An oscilloscope for video and clock signals
- A logic probe
- A Spectrum diagnostic ROM (Retroleum Smart Card or Brian’s diagnostic burned to a 27C128)
- A known good regulated 9V DC centre negative bench PSU at 1.5A or more

## 3. Safety First

The Spectrum PSU is low voltage, so there is no mains hazard at the board itself, but take care:

- Never work on a live board except when taking measurements.
- The 7805 regulator runs very hot in normal operation. Burns are easy. Don't run for longer than a few seconds without the heatsink attached. 
- If using a substitute PSU, verify polarity (original is centre negative) and voltage before plugging in. A reversed polarity PSU will destroy the board in seconds.

Removing DIP chips from these boards demands patience, as the substrate and copper bonding have often degraded with age. After desoldering each pin with a desoldering gun, pump, or wick, a small amount of solder almost always remains clinging to the pins and via barrels. Attempting to lever the chip out at this stage is the silent killer of old boards: it is frequently enough to tear pads from the substrate, lift traces, or pull plated barrels straight out of their vias. A safe approach is the gentle application of heat from a heat gun to the underside of the board, softening any residual solder across all pins simultaneously so the chip lifts out with minimal upward force. Keep the heat moderate and moving, and the touch light, as too much heat in one spot will lift pads on its own.

## 4. Initial Physical Checks

Before any power is applied, do a thorough visual inspection:

1. Open the case. On rubber key models, five screws on the base. The keyboard membrane tails are very fragile, so take care when lifting the top shell and do not crease them.
2. Inspect the PCB for obvious damage: burn marks, bulged or leaking capacitors, lifted traces, broken solder joints, corrosion near the edge connector, RF output, or power input.
3. Check all socketed chips are seated properly. Press each one down gently. The ULA, and sometimes the Z80 & ROM are socketed. Some issues also socket some of the RAM.
4. Look for bent pins on any socketed chip.
5. Check the keyboard membrane tails for cracking or oxidation on the exposed pads.
6. Inspect the RF modulator can for physical damage, especially if any previous work has been done.
7. Verify chip orientation. The notch or dot on each chip should align with the notch on the socket and the silkscreen on the PCB. A reversed chip at power on is usually instantly fatal.

## 5. Pre-Power Continuity Checks

With the machine unplugged, use the multimeter continuity function:

1. **Check the 5V rail**: Probe from the output leg of the 7805 to ground. You should see some resistance in both directions once the filter capacitors have settled, not a dead short. A hard short to ground points to a shorted electrolytic or a dead chip, often the ULA or a RAM chip. Do not power up if shorted.
2. **Check the 12V and -5V rails**: These are generated by the TR4/TR5 stage and feed the 4116 DRAM chips. Shorts here usually mean a failed transistor often resulting in dead lower RAM.
3. **Check the edge connector** between adjacent pins if the machine has previously been used with peripherals.

## 6. The TR4 and TR5 Power Transistor Test

This is the single most important pre-power check on any Spectrum. The 4116 DRAM chips need +5V, -5V, and +12V to operate correctly. The negative and positive auxiliary rails are generated by a small inverter circuit around TR4 (typically a ZTX650 NPN) and TR5 (typically a ZTX750 PNP), along with an inductor and a few small capacitors.

When TR4 or TR5 fails, one or both auxiliary rails is lost. The 4116 chips are then powered with only +5V present and can be damaged if left in this state. This is why neglected Spectrums often show cascading RAM failures: a marginal TR4 or TR5 slowly kills the DRAM every time the machine is switched on.

**Test procedure with power off:**

1. Identify TR4 and TR5 on the board, small TO-92 plastic transistors near the RAM area.
2. Set the multimeter to diode test. Measure base to emitter and base to collector on each transistor. You should see a diode drop in one direction and open circuit in the other. Any short between legs means a failed transistor.
3. If a transistor looks suspect in circuit, desolder and re-test out of circuit to confirm.

**Test procedure with power on (only if the off-state checks passed):**

1. Apply power very briefly and measure the 12V rail at pin 8 of any 4116.
2. Measure the -5V rail at pin 1 of any 4116.
3. If either rail is missing, badly out of tolerance, or oscillating audibly, power off immediately.

This test applies equally to every Issue from 1 through 6A and the 48K+. They all share the same three-rail arrangement.

## 7. First Power-On

Assuming continuity checks have passed and the TR4/TR5 pair test good:

1. Connect the RF output to a TV or an RF-capable device, or composite output if a composite mod has already been fitted.
2. Apply power. Note that there is no power LED on the machine, so you rely on the screen to tell you it is alive.
3. The screen should show a white border around a black paper area, followed by the familiar copyright sign-on message appearing shortly after power-on.
4. Listen for any squealing from the PSU or from the board. A very faint hiss from the power area is normal, a loud whine or scream is not.
5. Feel the 7805 heatsink after about thirty seconds. It runs hot, but should not be instantly too hot to touch. If it is, power off. You have a current draw problem.

If you see the normal sign-on screen, the machine is basically alive. If not, read on.

## 8. Symptom to Fault Mapping

### Completely dead, no display

- PSU fault or wrong polarity.
- 7805 failed short or open.
- Broken trace at the power input jack, often from years of plug wiggling.
- Open series diode on the input.

### Black screen, no picture or no sync

- Z80 not running. Check the 3.5MHz clock at pin 6 of the Z80.
- ULA fault (the expensive scenario, see section 10).
- ROM fault or ROM not seated properly.
- RESET line held low. Check the reset circuit and any peripheral on the edge connector.

### Solid coloured screen (pink, magenta, cyan, yellow, or similar)

- The ULA is running and generating video, but the CPU is not fetching and executing ROM correctly, or the system cannot even set up its initial workspace in lower RAM.
- Usually a ROM fault, a CPU fault, or a lower 16K RAM fault. The lower 16K is where both the display memory and the system variables live, so a fault in that area frequently prevents boot entirely and leaves whatever colour the ULA defaults to on screen.

### Sign-on screen appears but with garbage characters or corrupted pixels in the display area

- A fault in the lower 16K RAM, specifically in the display memory region. Display memory sits in the lower 16K, so visible pixel corruption points to a bit fault in that bank. Use the pixel counting method in section 9 to identify the faulty chip.

### Sign-on screen looks clean, but some BASIC programs crash, games fail to load, or memory tests fail

- A fault in the upper 32K RAM. The machine boots normally because the boot process only needs the lower 16K, so the clean sign-on tells you nothing about the upper bank. Diagnosis requires running memory test software, a diagnostic ROM, or POKE/PEEK tests at high addresses. More on this in section 9.

### Boots then crashes, or random resets

- Marginal RAM, often heat sensitive. Provoke the fault with a hairdryer or freeze spray and watch for behaviour changes.
- Failing electrolytic on a power rail, sagging under load.
- Keyboard short from a damaged membrane pulling a row or column that happens to hit BREAK or another critical key.

### Wavy, tearing, or unstable picture

- Modulator fault or ageing coils.
- Poor video ground.
- Master 14MHz crystal drifting (rare).
- ULA overheating.

### Thin horizontal lines, screen offset, or tearing

- Suspect ULA or master crystal.

## 9. RAM Fault Diagnosis

### Lower 16K: the pixel counting method

Because display memory lives in the lower 16K, a stuck bit in lower RAM is visible as a repeating pattern on the sign-on screen. Each byte of display memory represents 8 horizontal pixels of an 8-pixel-wide cell, and each bit of that byte corresponds to a single physical RAM chip.

**The method:**

1. Look at any clear character on screen, for example a letter in the sign-on message.
2. Count the pixel columns from left to right within each 8-pixel-wide cell.
3. If column 1 is consistently wrong across many characters, bit 7 is bad. If column 8 is wrong, bit 0 is bad.
4. The chip corresponding to that bit is the faulty one.

The bit-to-chip mapping varies by issue, so always confirm against the schematic for your specific board. Silkscreen numbering is not consistent across issues.

Confirmation methods:

- Swap-testing against a known good 4116 is the fastest confirmation. Keep a stock of pulled tested chips if you do this often.
- Piggyback testing (placing a known good 4116 on top of the suspect chip with pins aligned) will reveal a stuck-open fault, but will not help with a stuck-short fault because the working chip cannot overcome the shorted one.
- A diagnostic ROM cartridge reports the failed bit directly.

### Lower 16K total failure

If a lower RAM chip is completely dead rather than intermittently bad, the machine will often fail to boot at all and sit on a solid colour screen because the stack, system variables, and screen cannot be used.

### Upper 32K

The upper 32K holds loaded games, and the BASIC workspace, but not the display. A fault here cannot be diagnosed from the startup screen because the sign-on does not use upper RAM. Symptoms include:

- Startup looks clean, then attempts to run programs crash or behave erratically.
- Tape games fail to load or corrupt immediately on starting.
- RAMTOP tests and memory test programs report errors at high addresses.
- PRINT PEEK at high addresses returns wrong or inconsistent values.

Practical upper RAM diagnosis usually needs:

- A diagnostic ROM that walks a test pattern through the upper bank and reports failed bits and their likely chip.
- A BASIC memory test program loaded from tape or SD card.
- An oscilloscope or logic probe to chase activity on the data bus when accessing upper RAM addresses.

Once the failed bit is identified, the chip is found by the same bit-to-chip correspondence used for lower RAM, referenced against the schematic for your issue.

## 10. ULA Faults

The Ferranti ULA handles video generation, keyboard scanning, tape I/O, and the border. There are several variants across the production run:

- 5C102E: earliest, very rare.
- 5C112E: early.
- 6C001E: common, later.

A dead ULA is one of the most expensive faults on a Spectrum because these chips are hard to find and the price is high. Symptoms of a failed ULA:

- No video at all, while the CPU appears to run.
- Distorted or garbled video, random stripes, or persistent attribute corruption despite confirmed good RAM.
- Keyboard entirely dead with RAM, ROM, and CPU all confirmed good.
- Tape loading completely broken when everything else is fine.
- Overheating ULA that distorts video when hot and recovers when cool.

Before blaming the ULA, exhaustively test the ROM, RAM, CPU, clock, and support logic. Replacement options are a salvaged original or the nebULA by Retroleum, an FPGA-based drop-in replacement that also improves colour clash timing.

## 11. CPU, ROM, and Support Chip Faults

### Z80 CPU

Failures are uncommon but possible. Symptoms:

- Completely dead board with good power rails and a working 3.5MHz clock at pin 6.
- Random crashes that track with CPU temperature rather than any other chip.

Replacement Z80s are cheap and plentiful, and CMOS versions work fine.

### ROM

The Spectrum 48K ROM is a 16K mask ROM, typically a 2364 or pin-compatible part. Symptoms of a bad ROM:

- Solid colour screen at power on.
- No boot at all.
- Scrambled sign-on if only some bits are bad.

You can read the ROM in an EPROM programmer with a suitable socket adapter and compare the checksum against the known good image. Replacement with a 27C128 EPROM and a small adapter board is a well-trodden path.

### Support logic (74 series)

Boards carry several 74LS chips around the CPU and memory decoding. Failures here cause odd symptoms:

- Address decode faults causing memory mirroring.
- Bus contention showing as garbage at specific addresses.
- Clock glitches causing random crashes.

Diagnosis usually needs a logic probe or oscilloscope, and comparison against a known good machine is invaluable.

## 12. Keyboard Membrane

The rubber key membrane is a consumable. Original membranes from the 1980s are now all brittle and almost always cracked at the tail flex points. Symptoms:

- Specific keys unresponsive.
- Whole rows or columns dead (tail fracture).
- Ghost key presses (tracking corrosion or a fold in the tail).

Replacement membranes are readily available and cheap. Fitting is straightforward, the main risk is creasing the new tails during installation.

## 13. Capacitor Replacement

Electrolytic capacitors on the power rails are a common source of intermittent faults, especially the larger reservoir caps near the 7805. A conservative recap approach:

- Replace electrolytics on the power rails with modern low ESR equivalents of the same capacitance and voltage or higher.
- Leave original caps in signal paths if they test good on an ESR meter.
- Any cap showing bulging, leakage, or a high ESR reading should be replaced.

The Spectrum does not use tantalum capacitors, so there is no need to look for them.

## 14. Common Quality of Life Mods

### Composite video mod

The RF output is poor by modern standards, and many modern displays no longer accept RF at all. A composite mod gives a clean video signal through a phono jack.

The simplest approach:

Disconnect the cable from the PCB inside the modulator to the centre pin of the RCA jack output. 
Install a 100uF capacitor between the signal imput to the modulator and the centre pin you just disconnected. 

### Heatsinks

- **7805 regulator**: The original folded sheet metal heatsink is marginal. A larger TO-220 heatsink with thermal compound reduces running temperature and extends the life of the regulator and the caps around it. Fitting it in the rubber key case needs care as vertical clearance is tight.
- **ULA**: A stick-on aluminium heatsink on the ULA will not physically fit inside the rubber key case with the lid closed, so this mod is generally not practical on the 16K/48K original. On the 48K+ there is more room inside the wedge case and a low-profile heatsink can often be fitted.
- **DRAM**: Rarely needed, and the same case clearance problem applies on the rubber key original.

### Power supply replacement

Consider replacing the original linear PSU with a modern regulated 9V DC centre negative supply. The original supply is unregulated, which means its output voltage varies with both mains voltage and load. Under light load it can sit well above 9V, stressing the 7805 and the caps. A modern regulated supply holds the input voltage steady and runs the 7805 cooler.

### SD card interfaces

A DivMMC Future or similar SD card interface allows games to be loaded without tape, improving usability and avoiding wear on tape mechanisms.

### Joystick interfaces

A Kempston-compatible joystick interface is worth fitting for any machine that will be used for games.

## 15. Rare and Expensive Faults to Watch For

Some faults make a repair economically questionable unless the machine itself is a rare issue or has sentimental value.

### Dead ULA

As covered above. NOS ULAs are expensive and pulled ULAs carry their own risk. The nebULA is a sensible alternative.

### Issue 1 and Issue 5 boards

Issue 1 uses some parts that are genuinely difficult to source today, including specific upper RAM variants and an earlier ULA. Issue 5 is the rarest of the 48K family and commands very high prices among collectors. On either of these, repairs should be conservative and reversible to preserve value. Consider documenting the original configuration before any rework.

### Burned PCB traces

Severe power faults (wrong polarity PSU, a shorted capacitor that arced, a reversed chip at first power on) can burn traces under chips or through inner layers on later issues. Repair with bodge wires is possible, but visible PCB damage reduces collector value.

### Damaged edge connector

The rear edge connector pads are vulnerable to rough handling of peripherals and to corrosion from poor storage. Pad lifts on this connector are difficult to repair cleanly.

### ROM failure from a power event

Mask ROMs rarely bit rot in normal storage, but a severe power event can damage the die. Replacement with an EPROM in an adapter is straightforward but visibly non-original.

### Ferranti ULA with microcracks

A ULA that works cold and fails hot, or vice versa, often has a microcrack in a die bond or an internal trace. No repair is possible, only replacement.

## 16. A Suggested Troubleshooting Flow

1. Identify the board issue and do a full visual inspection. Do not rely on case style or serial number to tell you which board is inside.
2. Continuity check the power rails for shorts.
3. Test TR4 and TR5 using diode test before power on.
4. Apply power briefly and verify the 5V, 12V, and -5V rails.
5. If the screen is black, check Z80 clock, ROM, and RESET.
6. If solid colour screen, suspect lower RAM, ROM, or CPU.
7. If sign-on appears with pixel corruption, pixel count to find the faulty lower RAM chip.
8. If sign-on looks clean but programs crash, suspect upper RAM and diagnose with test software or a diagnostic ROM.
9. If the machine boots then crashes intermittently, suspect marginal RAM, electrolytic caps, or keyboard shorts.
10. If video is bad but programs run, suspect modulator, crystal, or ULA.
11. Only after exhausting cheaper causes should you suspect the ULA.

## 17. Reference Material and Working Habits

Keep a copy of the schematic for the board issue you are working on. Service manuals for the common issues are freely available online. Knowing which Z80 pin connects to which bus line, and which chip holds which bit, saves many hours of probing.

A known good Spectrum in the same workshop is invaluable both for comparison oscilloscope traces and for swap-testing socketed chips. When bringing a repaired machine up after any significant rework, use a bench PSU with a current limit set to around 500mA before trusting it on a full wall PSU.

Be mindful about the use of sockets and heatsinks. Modern hobbyist DIP board builds often have every chip socketed with a heatsink attached as a matter of course. Sockets certainly make troubleshooting easier, but consider this: if you have built the board properly, verified the voltage rails, checked for shorts, and tested each chip in an external tester beforehand, does that chip genuinely need to sit in a socket under a heatsink? Most RAM and logic ICs of the DIP era run perfectly cool in normal operation and were never designed with additional cooling in mind; a heatsink on a 74-series logic chip or a vintage DRAM is almost always solving a problem that does not exist. Expensive or rare chips are a different matter: something like a ULA is irreplaceable and runs notoriously hot, so a socket makes sense and a heatsink is well worth fitting where space allows, though on rubber-key machines the case clearance often rules this out. 

Chips are frequently cheaper than the sockets that hold them, and every socket introduces an additional failure point on top of the chip and the board itself. My advice: think twice before reaching for one.
